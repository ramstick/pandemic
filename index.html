I have a lot a free time currently, so I have coded up a few simple simulations for pandemics. There are many ways to simulate a pandemic, each having their pros and cons.

SIR Model

The SIR model is a simplistic model of a pandemic. It keeps track of 3 variables, the Susceptable (S), the Infected (I), and the Removed / Recovered (R). The Susceptable variable includes people who are not infectious, but are able to be infected. The Infected variable includes people who are infectious and can spread the disease. The Removed variable refers to the portion of the population who can no longer be infected and can not spread the disease. This model is a bit misleading, as the Removed variable would include the dead people, as they are no longer infectious and cannot be infected.

Herd Immunity

I'm not sure what the required amount for Herd Immunity is for Coronavirus. Last estimate I remember is 60% by the British Government. I am sure that it is improbable that we will be able to reach that level, as from the CSIRD model, 20% of the population ended up dead. I did the math and 7 billion times 20% is more than a billion. Now my simulation is far from perfect for modeling a pandemic, but Herd Immunity looks like an unreachable position without vaccines.

Conclusions

The best way to end the pandemic is to lower the Infectivity of the disease. The models above clearly show evidence for this. Due to the high infectivity of the virus, even physical contact or talking could spread the virus. Currently the best way to lower the Infectivity and slow the spread is to social distance. Many countries and states are currently in lockdown mode in an attempt to make more people social distance and to decrease the amount of physical interactions that occur, but ultimately the choice is up to you.
